The Swiss Landscape

Switzerland's structured reporting is built on climate and sustainability.

Unlike the EU, Switzerland does not mandate ESEF for its domestic listings. But since January 2024, large Swiss companies must publish climate disclosures in both human-readable and machine-readable formats. The Federal Council's 2026 revisions align Swiss reporting with ESRS and IFRS S2, and a proposed Swiss equivalent to CSRD will extend these obligations to mid-sized firms.

PDF-only disclosures

Legacy · Unstructured

Swiss non-financial reports have traditionally been published as PDFs on company websites. Readable, but not comparable or automatable. No machine-readable layer for regulators, investors or analysts.

XBRL machine-readable

2024+ · Climate Ordinance

The Swiss Climate Ordinance requires reports in both human-readable AND machine-readable (XBRL) formats on company websites. Enables cross-border comparability with EU ESRS and IFRS S2 disclosures.

Who is affected today? Public companies, banks and insurance companies with 500+ employees AND either CHF 20M+ total assets or CHF 40M+ turnover. First reports covered FY 2024 and were published in 2025. The proposed Swiss CSRD alignment (under consultation, expected 1 January 2026) would lower thresholds significantly to 250 FTEs, CHF 25M assets, or CHF 50M turnover — dramatically expanding scope.

Frequently Asked Questions

Switzerland XBRL — your questions answered.

Does Switzerland have an ESEF mandate like the EU?

No — Switzerland is not in the EU and does not impose ESEF on its domestic SIX Swiss Exchange listings. However, Swiss companies with secondary listings on EU-regulated markets (Frankfurt, Paris, Amsterdam, etc.) must file ESEF iXBRL to the relevant EU Officially Appointed Mechanism (OAM).

Who must file under the Swiss Climate Ordinance?

Public companies, banks and insurance companies with 500+ employees AND at least one of: CHF 20 million or more in total assets, or CHF 40 million or more in turnover. The obligation came into force on 1 January 2024. Disclosures must be in both human-readable and machine-readable (XBRL) format on the company website.

What is changing with the revised Climate Ordinance in 2026?

On 6 December 2024, the Federal Council opened a consultation to revise the Climate Ordinance. The TCFD framework is being replaced by acceptance of equivalent international standards — specifically ESRS and IFRS S2. The revised ordinance is expected to enter into force on 1 January 2026.

What is the proposed Swiss CSRD alignment?

On 26 June 2024, the Federal Council proposed aligning CO Art. 964a-c with the EU CSRD. The proposed thresholds are significantly lower: any two of 250 FTEs, CHF 25M balance sheet total, or CHF 50M revenue triggers reporting obligations. Expected entry into force: 1 January 2026, pending legislative outcome.

What is XBRL CH and is it mandatory?

XBRL CH is the national Swiss XBRL taxonomy developed by XBRL Switzerland, supporting financial reporting under the Swiss Code of Obligations (OR), Swiss GAAP FER, and IFRS. It is currently voluntary — no Swiss authority has mandated it for financial statement filing.

What does FINMA Circular 2026/1 require?

FINMA Circular 2026/1 on "Nature-related financial risks" came into force on 1 January 2026. It sets out FINMA's expectations regarding how banks and insurers manage climate-related and broader nature-related financial risks, applying proportionality for larger institutions.

Do I need both human-readable and machine-readable versions?

Yes. The Swiss Climate Ordinance requires the disclosures to be made available in both formats on the company's website — a human-readable PDF for stakeholders, and a machine-readable XBRL file for automated processing. XBRtags delivers both as part of every climate engagement.

What is double-materiality and why does Switzerland require it?

Double-materiality means disclosing both the financial risks climate poses to your company AND the impact your company has on the climate. Switzerland adopted double-materiality to align with the EU ESRS framework and give investors a fuller picture of corporate climate exposure.

What about sanctions for non-compliance?

Fines associated with Switzerland's Ordinance on Climate Disclosures are stipulated in Article 325ter of the Swiss Criminal Code. Non-compliance can result in financial penalties and reputational damage. XBRtags ensures your filings are technically compliant before delivery.
